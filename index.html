Research Experience

1. Multi-Omics Integration revealing the DLC1-FOXD3 Axis in Neural Crest Development

During my Master’s program and subsequent role as a research assistant, I worked on a project that uncovered a unique post-transcriptional regulatory mechanism between the protein DLC1 and FOXD3 mRNA. An overview of this work is described below.

While the RhoGAP protein DLC1 is known to regulate neural crest cell fate and migration by modulating downstream neural crest-determining markers like SOX9, SNAI2, and FOXD3, the precise mechanism governing FOXD3 regulation has remained elusive. Unlike SOX9 and SNAI2, which are regulated via intronic splicing, FOXD3 is an intronless gene, suggesting an alternative regulatory pathway. To elucidate this, we performed mass spectrometry following DLC1 immunoprecipitation (IP-MS) in iPSC-derived neural crest cells. Functional enrichment of DLC1 cofactors revealed a novel role for DLC1 in mRNA stabilization through direct binding. This interaction was mapped using DLC1 RIP-seq, which demonstrated a distinct enrichment of binding signals localized around the 3' UTR of FOXD3. Actinomycin D-treated RNA decay assays confirmed that DLC1 knockdown significantly accelerates FOXD3 mRNA degradation, proving its stabilizing role during normal neural crest development. To determine how DLC1 selectively recognizes its targets, we integrated differential expression data from RNA-seq (DLC1 knockdown vs. control) with our RIP-seq data. Motif analysis of down-regulated transcripts harboring DLC1 3' UTR peaks uncovered a highly conserved sequence motif, indicating a clear sequence-specific recognition preference for DLC1. Finally, clinical database mining revealed concurrent downregulation of both DLC1 and FOXD3 in iPSC-derived neural crest cells from Hirschsprung disease patients, underscoring the potential pathogenic risk of disrupting this post-transcriptional regulatory axis during early development.

2. Spatial Mapping Algorithms for Single-Cell Transcriptomics of the PMBCs

Traditional computational methods for inferring cell-cell communication (CCC) are constrained by a critical data modality mismatch. Current frameworks rely either entirely on single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq)—which captures detailed ligand-receptor (LR) signaling profiles in fluidic, unstructured samples like blood—or entirely on spatial transcriptomics (ST), which provides highly accurate localized predictions within fixed solid tissues. However, a major unmet research gap exists in modeling the dynamic endocrine cross-talk occurring at the interface of these two environments: specifically, how to analyze CCC between a solid tissue and the mobile blood cells flowing through its internal vasculature. This limitation is particularly evident in diseases like diabetic retinopathy, where crucial pathogenic immune signals are exchanged between the fixed retinal tissue and peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) circulating through passing vessels. Prior to this work, no computational framework could effectively leverage both the spatial coordinates of ST tissue data and the fluidic cell profiles of blood scRNA-seq data simultaneously.

  • Methodology & Logic: The Probabilistic "Pseudo Distance" Model

To bridge this gap, we developed a novel probabilistic model that introduces a spatial framework to circulating blood cells. Because flowing PBMCs lack precise coordinate data, the model operates on the logical assumption that a blood cell’s spatial probability distribution matches the density of local blood vessels within the tissue.

  1. Vascular Mapping: We first deconvolute the solid tissue's ST data to calculate the local proportion of endothelial cells at every spot, establishing a normalized vascular density function ($f_{endo}$).
  2. Pseudo Distance Calculation: Using this density function as a spatial probability map, we calculate an expected mathematical "pseudo distance" between each fixed tissue cell and the circulating blood cells, weighting their physical geometric distances by localized vascular probabilities.
  3. Network & Regulatory Scoring: By integrating this pseudo distance into a modified stMLnet framework, we score LR signaling activities inversely to their proximity to the blood vessels. We then employ Random Forest Regression to quantify how strongly these spatial signaling activities regulate downstream target gene expression, filtering out low-importance pairs to isolate true endocrine interactions.
  • Key Findings in Retina-PBMC Data

Applying this model to diabetic retinopathy data revealed that multiple Müller glia-secreted ligands, including VIM, SPP1, and COL9A2, aberrantly converge on the CD44 receptor of B cells. This interaction drives a highly active, self-sustaining reciprocal feedback loop with B-cell-derived NPG1 targeting Müller glia ERBB2 receptors, revealing a novel pathogenic mechanism that sustains chronic inflammation in the diabetic ocular microenvironment.
