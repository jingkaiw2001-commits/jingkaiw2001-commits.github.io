Research Experience

1. Multi-Omics Integration revealing the DLC1-FOXD3 Axis in Neural Crest Development

During my Master’s program and subsequent role as a research assistant, I worked on a project that uncovered a unique post-transcriptional regulatory mechanism between the protein DLC1 and FOXD3 mRNA. An overview of this work is described below.

Background & The Knowledge Gap

  • Our Previous Discovery: In our recent co-authored study (Zheng et al., 2025, Nature Communications), we demonstrated that the RhoGAP protein DLC1 functions within a splicing complex to determine trunk neural crest cell fate by directly regulating the intronic splicing of key markers SOX9 and SNAI2.
  • The Missing Link: Crucially, while SOX9 and SNAI2 rely on this intronic splicing mechanism, FOXD3 is an intronless gene. The precise mechanism governing how DLC1 regulates FOXD3 has remained entirely elusive, pointing toward a completely alternative regulatory pathway which this project aims to resolve.

Mechanistic Discovery & Validation

  • Uncovering a New Role: Immunoprecipitation mass spectrometry (IP-MS) for DLC1 cofactors in iPSC-derived neural crest cells unexpectedly revealed a novel, direct role for DLC1 in mRNA stabilization.
  • Mapping the Interaction: RNA immunoprecipitation sequencing (RIP-seq) demonstrated a distinct enrichment of DLC1 binding signals specifically localized around the 3' UTR of FOXD3.
  • Functional Proof: Actinomycin D-treated RNA decay assays confirmed that DLC1 knockdown significantly accelerates FOXD3 mRNA degradation, proving its essential stabilizing role during normal neural crest development.

Omics data Integration Discovered Sequence-Specific Target Recognition

To determine how DLC1 selectively recognizes its targets, we integrated differential expression data from RNA-seq (DLC1 knockdown vs. control) with our RIP-seq dataset. Motif analysis of down-regulated genes harboring DLC1 3' UTR peaks uncovered a highly conserved sequence motif, demonstrating a clear sequence-specific recognition preference for DLC1 target binding.

Clinical Relevance

Clinical database mining revealed a concurrent downregulation of both DLC1 and FOXD3 in iPSC-derived neural crest cells from Hirschsprung disease patients. This underscores the potential pathogenic risk of disrupting this novel post-transcriptional regulatory axis during early human embryonic development.

2. Bridging Solid Tissue and Circulating Immunity: A Spatial Cross-Talk Framework

During my research internship at Sun Yat-sen University, I addressed a fundamental limitation in cell-cell communication modeling by developing an innovative computational framework designed to interface solid tissue microenvironments with fluidic, circulating blood cell profiles.

Background & The Knowledge Gap - Cross-talk between solid tissue and circulating blood cells

Traditional cell-cell communication tools rely exclusively on either scRNA-seq of cell suspension or spatial transcriptomics (ST) of fixed tissue sections, leaving a critical gap in modeling cross-talk between solid tissues and circulating blood cells. My work introduced a framework to map interactions between a retina section and blood (PBMCs), uncovering pathogenic immune signals previously hidden in diseases like diabetic retinopathy.

Methodology & Computational Logic

  • Vascular Density Mapping: The solid tissue's ST data is first deconvoluted to calculate the local proportion of endothelial cells at every spot, establishing a normalized vascular density function to establish spatial probability maps for flowing cells.
  • The "Pseudo Distance" Model: Operating on the logical principle that a blood cell’s spatial probability matches local vascular density, we calculate an expected mathematical "pseudo distance" between each fixed tissue cell and circulating blood cells, weighting physical geometric distances by localized vascular probabilities.
  • Network Scoring & Filtering: Integrated these pseudo distances into a modified stMLnet framework to score ligand-receptor (LR) activities inversely to vascular proximity. Random Forest Regression was then deployed to quantify signaling regulatory strengths on downstream targets, filtering out low-importance pairs to isolate true endocrine interactions.

Key Findings & Biological Insight

  • Inflammatory Feedback Loop: Applying this model to diabetic retinopathy datasets revealed that multiple Müller glia-secreted ligands (including VIM, SPP1, and COL9A2) aberrantly converge on the CD44 receptor of circulating B cells.
  • Pathogenic Mechanism: This interaction drives a highly active, self-sustaining reciprocal feedback loop where B-cell-derived NPG1 targets Müller glia ERBB2 receptors, uncovering a novel pathogenic mechanism that sustains chronic inflammation in the diabetic ocular microenvironment.

3. Construction of a Multi-Omics Regulatory Network of ADHD via Mendelian Randomization

Driven by a longstanding interest in psychiatric genetics, I explored causal inference frameworks to advance target prediction boundaries for Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD), a highly heritable neurodevelopmental condition.

Background & The Genetic Challenge

  • The Disease Complexity: ADHD is a complex polygenic disorder influenced by numerous risk variants across the genome. While large-scale GWAS have identified many associated loci, these associations are often confounded, leaving the direct causality between these risk mutations and ADHD unclear.
  • Methodological Opportunity: By leveraging Mendelian randomization (MR), naturally randomized genetic variants (SNPs) can be used as instrumental variables. This bypasses environmental confounding to establish rigorous causal associations between any phenotypes associated with SNPs and the disease.

Multi-Omics Network Integration

  • Ecosystem Construction: We extended standard transcriptome-wide association principles into a comprehensive multi-omics ecosystem by integrating multi-tissue quantitative trait loci (QTL) summary statistics with large-scale ADHD genome-wide association study (GWAS) data.
  • Alternative Splicing Capture: Beyond mapping simple gene expression (via eQTL), the architecture explicitly captured disease-associated splicing events (via sQTL), mapping specific alternative splicing perturbations that directly disrupt early neurodevelopmental pathways.
  • Epigenetic Layering: Recognizing that postnatal environmental dynamics alter psychiatric phenotypes through epigenetic modifications, DNA methylation QTL (mQTL) datasets were seamlessly integrated to construct a multi-layered, cascading regulatory chain (mQTL → eQTL/sQTL → ADHD).

Clinical Significance & Target Prioritization

This integrative multi-layered network mapped post-natal pathogenic mechanisms, providing a significantly deeper, mechanistic understanding of ADHD etiology and potential therapeutic target. This paper was peer-reviewed and published in Briefings in Bioinformatics (DOI: 10.1093/bib/bbae502).
